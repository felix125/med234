Around 2008 or 2009, a doctor at the Taipei Veterans General Hospital asked if I would like to participate in a research study. They had a new drug that could be used to treat Hepatitis B, and I immediately agreed. The doctor arranged for several tests and told me to return for my next appointment, where I would be given the new medication.

A week quickly passed, and when I returned for my appointment, I asked the doctor about the test results.

"Good news, you don't need to participate in the study," the doctor said. <br />
"Why? How is that good news?"</br />
"Your Hepatitis B virus has almost disappeared. Your viral load is so low that it's almost undetectable, so you can't participate in the study. I estimate that your virus will be completely gone in a few months, so congratulations."

I was stunned. I had been infected with Hepatitis B since birth, but the virus was not active, non-infectious, and did not require aggressive treatment. There were no medications for it. When I graduated from college, I was even once suspected of having liver cancer due to high levels of Alpha-Fetoprotein, which frightened my whole family.

I thought I would be a lifelong carrier, and whenever I passed by a blood donation vehicle, I would feel a sense of loss: "I want to donate blood, but I can't!"

However, no doctor had ever told me that Hepatitis B could heal naturally, and yet it had happened. I asked the doctor for further clarification, and he explained: "This is because your body has entered the immune clearance phase. There are indeed clinical cases like this." I suddenly understood!

This phenomenon can also happen with cancer, where cancer cells are inexplicably cleared by the immune system, but the medical field still does not understand this mechanism.

Wait a minute, why did I accept this theory so easily? When the doctor told me this was due to the "immune clearance phase," why did I believe it? Isn't this just a description of a phenomenon? It's like saying, "Ah! The sun sets," without explaining anything. Or did adding a mysterious term to the setting sun make it more convincing? Why is a description treated as an explanation?

1. The Authority of Language
When a phenomenon is described using technical terms such as "immune clearance" or "immune escape," people often believe these terms are explanations in themselves because of the unequal relationship between knowledge and power. However, most of these terms merely describe an observation. The professionalism and authority of medical language often cause people to overlook the deeper question of "why this happens."

2. Resistance to the 'Unknown'
For many people, especially those like me, acknowledging the "unknown" in a phenomenon is difficult. The medical field may be inclined to fill in the gaps with existing knowledge, even if these explanations are simply descriptions, because in the scientific context, offering an "explanation" is often more persuasive than admitting "I don't know."

3. Practical Considerations
If the description of a phenomenon is sufficient to solve a problem, then the description can be accepted as a "practical explanation."

However, this doesn't explain all phenomena. I feel there is more to explore. Despite the medical field's lack of understanding of the underlying causes, descriptions from authority figures seem to explain everything. This reinforces the position of medicine and turns more "unknown situations" into "known categories" within the field, which, to some extent, limits other possible explanations. This kind of language context narrows the space for alternative interpretations and erases all possibilities from life itself or from any efforts I may have made.

What confuses me even more is that the description of the "immune clearance phase" itself cannot be proven. Since I no longer have the Hepatitis B virus, medical science cannot conduct any further research on my case! Many unresolved diseases, such as cancer, face similar challenges. Medicine can study diseases, but it cannot study health, nor can it study how those who naturally recover return to health!

In contrast to marginalized cultures, this is the inherent advantage of dominant cultures! Within this linguistic context, medicine becomes a necessary, even the only, choice. Even though everyone knows that people with terminal illnesses must change their mindset and lifestyle, medical intervention has become an unavoidable measure, and morally necessary.

Therefore, I think we need not only to treat but also to heal ourselves. Sometimes, medicine abandons the patient when no solutions are found, and we must go beyond medicine. When it comes to life, medicine's understanding is still quite limited. By recognizing all of our potential gifts, we might discover many paths that have yet to be explored.
