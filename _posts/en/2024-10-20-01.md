During my graduate school years, I once caught a terrible cold that left me feverish, weak, and utterly drained. I visited a medical center in Taoyuan twice within two weeks, but my condition showed no signs of recovery. I couldn't help but wonder how a simple cold could defy all treatments.

Frustrated and desperate, a voice inside me urged, "Go back to your hometown, Yilan, and visit your family doctor." Without a second thought, I hastily packed up and rushed to the train station without even informing my lab of my leave.

Upon arriving in Yilan, I didn't even unpack my bags. I immediately headed to the clinic. My family doctor greeted me with his usual warm smile. He was calm, experienced, and always seemed reassuringly in control. His desk held two computers, one for patient records and the other for reading medical journals, a rarity among clinic doctors I'd met.

He checked my temperature, listened to my heart, lungs, and stomach sounds with his stethoscope, and carefully examined my throat, eyes, and mouth. After a thorough check-up, he set down his tools and turned to me with a knowing look in his eyes. "It's been a while since I last gave you an IV drip. Come back this afternoon. You'll be fine." At that moment, I truly believed I was on the road to recovery. His confidence and assurance gave me such peace of mind that it felt like the cure had already begun.

I returned to the clinic in the afternoon for the IV drip and went to bed early that night. To my surprise, I was fully recovered in just two days.

From the 1990s into the early 2000s, the rampant overuse of antibiotics in Taiwan was a common practice. Doctors often prescribed them in excessive amounts to meet patient expectations. Patients, especially those from working-class backgrounds, demanded fast-acting remedies to avoid missing work. Doctors who prescribed aggressively were deemed more competent, while those who didn’t were often criticized.

However, my family doctor stood out. He rarely prescribed more than four pills and often included vitamins among them. I only realized this after studying medicine myself. Intrigued, I asked him about his approach.

He explained, "The common cold is a viral infection, and there's no specific medication to cure it. Recovery mainly depends on the patient's immune system and self-healing ability. Overprescribing not only burdens the liver and kidneys but also doesn't provide real help."

Through our conversations, I gradually came to understand that the excessive prescribing of medications by many doctors at the time stemmed from two main factors. First, it was to meet public expectations. In that era, there was a prevailing belief that strong medicine was necessary to cure an illness, and that failing to completely eradicate a disease would only set the stage for future ailments. Second, doctors at the time had strong faith in antibiotics and the belief in preventive treatment, leading most doctors to prescribe antibiotics when treating a cold, sometimes even multiple types. Given the difficulty in distinguishing bacterial from viral infections clinically, the use of antibiotics felt reasonable. As a result, the beliefs of both doctors and patients formed a self-reinforcing cycle. Doctors who prescribed more and stronger medications gained more patients, income, and fame.

Back then, I had no concept of the placebo effect. But reflecting on this experience now, it is a textbook example of it.

The IV drip my doctor administered was nothing more than saline solution. The true "treatment" came from his warm, genuine demeanor, which reshaped my belief in my own recovery. From the moment I accepted the idea that I was being cured, my body responded accordingly. It was a powerful testament to the interplay between a doctor's character and a patient's mind.
