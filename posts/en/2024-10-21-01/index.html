Placebo: Fear Can Be Useful Too!

Author： Felix Chang
Published on： October 21, 2024

I have witnessed the placebo effect many times in the lives of my family and friends. One such instance occurred in 2007, when my girlfriend, Hong Zhuzhu1, and I were working at National Central University in Taiwan. We rented a small apartment near the school, living a peaceful and somewhat relaxed life, relying on the resources the school and its vicinity provided.

One day, Zhuzhu came home from the office, saying she felt dizzy and nauseous. I quickly drove her to the emergency department at Lixin Hospital.

After a brief consultation with the emergency doctor, who ordered an X-ray and blood tests, Zhuzhu was asked to lie on a hospital bed to rest. We waited a long time without any updates, possibly because the ER was busy, and the testing resources at the time were insufficient to meet the demand. The discomfort of the long wait was almost worse than the physical symptoms.

Finally, a nurse arrived holding a syringe as large as a Coca-Cola bottle. I could clearly see the size of the needle, and I was certain the injection would be incredibly painful. Zhuzhu was terrified, and she immediately told the nurse, “I’m feeling better, no need for the injection!” Her words surprised me, but I couldn’t help but laugh. Still, I carefully checked to confirm all her symptoms had truly disappeared before I spoke to the doctor. The doctor agreed that no injection was needed, and we were allowed to go home and rest.

After the doctor’s approval, Zhuzhu hurried out of the hospital as if escaping from a nightmare. On the way back, we felt very relaxed, laughing and talking the entire time. All her symptoms had disappeared without a trace. She kept describing how terrifying the syringe looked, and I couldn’t help but laugh, saying, “Your fear cured you. Haha!” It turned out that fear could trigger the placebo effect!

Henry Beecher, a former U.S. Army doctor, once struggled to understand a phenomenon he witnessed. He saw many severely injured patients refuse painkillers but complain loudly about the pain of needle injections. What was the difference between pain and no pain? He thought: “Strong emotions can block pain, and this is a common experience. From the soldiers’ perspective, it’s important to consider that being wounded allowed them to escape a life threatening environment and gain access to a hospital, which they saw as a safe haven from exhaustion, discomfort, anxiety, fear, and the threat of death. Their dilemma ended, at least, that’s how these soldiers saw it.”2,3

The belief of these soldiers, which triggered emotional and physiological responses, mirrors the same mechanism in Zhuzhu’s case. The placebo effect is truly magical. When faced with two forms of suffering, in order to avoid a more terrible outcome, the lesser affliction often leads to healing!
